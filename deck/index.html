ILEX

L’Interdit is French for “the forbidden.” We chose the name because the law denies this wine its origin. We built the whole experience around that denial, and this document explains how.

I

The Brief

An immersive experience for a heritage maison
that happens to have been born this year.

You asked us for a site that drips luxury and exudes scarcity: six thousand six hundred bottles of the first 0,5 % Grand Cru Classé-quality Bordeaux, sold direct, in a category that usually offers a dusty photograph of tied lavender.

“dream big here.”

The category

Non-alcoholic wine sells an apology: clinical bottles, functional promises, “all the taste, none of the…”. Luxury Bordeaux sells the assurance that nothing has changed since 1855.

Ilex fits neither shelf. It has something new to be proud of, and a legal reason it cannot brag: the moment the alcohol left, French law took the name away.

II

The Concept

Règlement (UE) 2021/2117 took the name.

A fully dealcoholised wine may not carry its protected designation of origin. At 0,5 % it is denied even Vin de France.

So: Grand Cru Classé fruit, limestone terroir, oak élevage, and a label forbidden from saying where any of it happened. Sans appellation.

We never had to invent a fiction. The provocation sits in the Official Journal of the European Union, and the appellation system plays the antagonist for free.

EXHIBIT A — LA LOI ORIGIN: Saint-Émilion, GIRONDE.
ESTATE: Franc Mayne, GRAND CRU CLASSÉ.
VARIETY: MERLOT NOIR, 100 PCT.
STATUS: DÉCLASSÉ — SANS APPELLATION
— seen, recorded, and rather enjoyed.

“The law forbids us to say
where this wine is from.
The wine, however, is under
no such obligation.”

l’interdit — the first estate proud to be declassified.

We printed the censorship.

Every mention of the origin appears on the site struck through in ink, in plain view. Around the redactions, the dossier documents parcel, soil, barrel and cellar in enough detail that you reach the verdict yourself.

Other luxury brands invented clubs and forged heritage to give their product a world. Every fact on this site is true, down to the regulation number. Only the name is missing — and a public register of 6,600 numbered bottles lets you verify the scarcity instead of taking our word for it.

IV

The Subtle Decisions

What we refused to do

We hide the menu until you step back.
No navigation on arrival, and none summonable. It appears only when you scroll up, away from the experience.
We animate the arrival once.
The arrival runs once: clouds, then the oak, then I·L·E·X letter by letter, then vines, thesis, crest. After that the page holds still.
We kept the scroll cue faint.
A single hairline chevron, arriving last in the sequence, drifting a few pixels. You can miss it unless you look for it.
We ration the gold.
Foil appears on bottle numbers, on seals, and on the closing line. Nowhere else.

The redaction system

We type the record on, word by word.
The dossier text arrives word by word, at a clerk’s cadence, and the black bars wipe across as the words are written.
Hold a redaction and the word shows through.
Press a redaction and nothing happens. Hold it, and the ink thins until the forbidden word shows in claret. Let go and it closes.
We made your highlighter into censor’s ink.
Select any text on the site with your cursor and the selection renders as censor’s ink. Try it on this sentence.
We were denied even “Vin de France”.
At 0,5 % the wine is denied even the lowest rung of the ladder, so the status line reads DÉCLASSÉ — SANS APPELLATION.

The numbers

We count the bottles up in front of you.
The 6 140 counts up fast, then decelerates until the last bottles arrive one at a time: …6 137, 6 138, 6 139.
We light the marks one at a time.
Each of the register’s 6,600 marks pops in on its own scattered timing over three seconds, so the field reads as individual bottles.
We let the number leave the frame.
In La Séparation, 14,2 % drains to 0,5 % under your scroll. Then the number scrolls up and out of the frame, leaving the dark, the bubbles, and one sentence.
We replaced the cart with a seal you hold.
There is no add-to-cart. You press the wax seal and hold it for a full second while a ring of gold closes, as one holds wax.
V

The Experience

Section by section.

01 · The Arrival

The arrival

The engraved world of the label assembles at architectural scale: sky, oak, wordmark, vines, in a single timed sequence.

  • The thesis greets you. No welcome copy, no tagline. The legal provocation, immediately.
  • Two doors: apply for the allocation, or read the dossier.
  • DÉCLASSÉ stamped where a heritage crest would boast.
02 · Le Dossier

We type the case over a drifting map

The scene pins. Behind everything, the wordless map of the right bank drifts imperceptibly — a 110-second survey. The record types itself, redactions inking in as it writes; then the prose slides aside and Exhibit A presents the law itself.

  • The map has no names. Its cartouche is empty, by order. The reader is trusted to know the river.
  • We document instead of describing. Parcel, soil, barrel and cellar, recorded the way a notary would.
03 · La Séparation

The night of 7 November

Your scroll drains the number — 14,2 % to 0,5 % — while bubbles rise across the full screen, in front of and behind the ghosted still. Then the number departs the frame, and two lines arrive alone:

The alcohol departs.
Its spirit remains.

04 · Le Registre

Six thousand six hundred marks

The page becomes a full-screen field of 6,600 marks — one per bottle, each igniting on its own clock. Hover any dot: its number, its status. Click a free one: it becomes yours to claim. The Acte d’Inscription rises over the field as the only door to purchase.

  • The register is public and permanent. Every keeper is inscribed by initials and city.
  • The inaugural allocation. Nothing has ever been sold before; 400 bottles rest with the chai.
05 · La Société des Déclassés

The exiled keep excellent company.

Membership by petition. First rights to every allocation; the proclamation each November on the anniversary of the Séparation; one dinner a year in a cellar whose address is never printed.

  • Founded MMXXV, the year the name was taken. The society is new and says so in Roman numerals.
  • We kept the shop quiet. One line and one button; no product grid competing with the wine.
06 · The Claim — the product page

The claim

The bottle stands alone on the parchment with a real shadow grounding it and the guardian oak ghosted behind. Beside it, the act:

  • Choose your number. It is yours alone, forever — or le greffier chooses…
  • Press and hold the seal. The gold ring closes; the claim is held twenty minutes in your name.
  • The evidence follows: procès-verbal de dégustation, fiche technique, livraison — all in the Law’s voice.

Why it matters

The register lets a customer verify the scarcity instead of taking our word for it. The redactions turn a legal setback into the detail people describe to their friends. The seal turns checkout into initiation. And a wine that cannot say where it is from hands every journalist their headline.

You will not find the words premium, exclusive or crafted anywhere on the site. The restraint makes that argument, and it is what lets a maison founded this year read like one that has kept a cellar for three hundred.
