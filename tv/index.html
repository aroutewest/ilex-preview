Our oak predates the appellation.

France founded the INAO in 1936 and began drawing legal borders around wine. Saint-Émilion wrote its first classification in 1955. A holm oak on limestone lives three to five hundred years. The institutions that refuse this wine a name are younger than the tree that shades its vines.

We go over the law’s head, to the one witness on the hill with seniority.

Alcohol departs.

The soul remains.

Our roots run deep, through soil and limestone.

The outcrop under the oak is the same limestone the vines drink from. A holm oak sends its taproot metres down through the beds and finds water there in the driest August, which is how it keeps its leaves when everything else on the hill has given up.

Masons call the stone calcaire à astéries, starfish limestone: the beds are packed with small sea creatures from thirty million years ago. What these roots grip is older than every institution in this story.

We call the shop le greffe.

French gives the word two meanings and we keep both: le greffe, the clerk’s office, and la greffe, the graft. Papers, seals, prints and other lasting things are propagated here from the mother tree, and they stay in stock while the wine itself is rationed.
